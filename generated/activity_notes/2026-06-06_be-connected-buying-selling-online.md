## 2026-06-06 20:22 - Sourced notes: buying and selling online

### Sourced overview
- Buying and selling online can be convenient, but learners need both consumer-rights knowledge and scam-safety habits.
- The strongest Australian teaching sources for this topic are:
  - ACCC for consumer rights and refunds
  - Scamwatch for scam warning signs
  - eSafety / Be Connected for simple online safety habits and seller tips

### Key sourced teaching points

#### 1. Online shopping has the same basic consumer protections as in-person shopping
- The ACCC says online businesses have the same responsibilities as physical businesses.
- If an overseas business sells directly to consumers in Australia, it must also follow Australian Consumer Law.
- However, it can still be harder in practice to get a repair, replacement, or refund from some overseas sellers.

#### 2. Marketplaces are different from ordinary online shops
- The ACCC explains that on marketplaces such as eBay, the consumer guarantee rights usually apply to the seller, not the platform.
- That means learners need to understand who they are really buying from.
- If the seller is a private individual rather than a business, some consumer guarantees may not apply.

#### 3. Before buying, learners should check whether the seller looks real and reachable
- ACCC advice highlights checking for:
  - a good reputation or reviews
  - clear refund / replacement information
  - clear systems for protecting personal and financial information
  - contact details such as a phone number, physical address, and business registration details
- Scamwatch also says missing contact information, no privacy policy, and no terms and conditions are warning signs.

#### 4. Scam warning signs should be taught explicitly
- Scamwatch says some red flags include:
  - prices that are unbelievably low
  - fake or cloned websites
  - suspicious web addresses
  - pressure to pay in unusual ways
  - buyers or sellers who rush the transaction
- eSafety says scammers may look like real shops, banks, or delivery services, so learners should think critically even when something looks professional.

#### 5. Safer payment methods matter
- ACCC notes that card providers may be able to reverse some charges if a product or service is not received, depending on the situation and time limits.
- ACCC also notes that services such as PayPal may offer refunds in some cases if the seller does not resolve the issue.
- Be Connected and eSafety both present secure services such as PayPal or credit card as safer than unusual payment methods.
- Scamwatch warns that scammers may prefer money orders, gift cards, wire transfers, bitcoin, or direct arrangements outside the platform.

#### 6. Learners should keep records
- ACCC says consumers should keep copies of receipts, emails, item descriptions, and other electronic records.
- This is a strong practical consumer-safety habit for class emphasis.

#### 7. Selling online also has risks
- Be Connected notes that sellers should:
  - start with low-value items while learning the platform
  - write clear, honest descriptions
  - use good photos
  - avoid revealing personal details in photos
  - meet buyers in safe public places where possible
  - avoid handing over an item before full payment is confirmed
  - avoid moving the transaction outside the selling platform
- Scamwatch also warns about fake buyers who offer to buy expensive items without viewing them, or who ask the seller to pay upfront transport or other fees.

### Practical class-ready safety rules
- Buy from sellers you can identify and contact.
- Check the web address carefully.
- Look for `https` and the padlock, but explain that these signs alone do not prove a seller is trustworthy.
- Read refund, return, and delivery information before paying.
- Use payment methods with stronger protections where possible.
- Keep screenshots, confirmations, receipts, and tracking details.
- Never rush because of pressure, countdown timers, or “today only” messages.
- For selling, keep communication inside the selling platform and confirm payment before handing anything over.

### Good discussion questions
- What is the difference between buying from a business and buying from a private seller?
- Why might a very cheap product sometimes be a warning sign?
- What details would help you decide whether a seller is trustworthy?
- Why is it safer to keep messages and payment inside a known platform?
- What should you do first if you think you have been scammed?

### Follow-up activity ideas
- Compare a safe-looking and unsafe-looking shopping site.
- Check a fake example web address for warning signs.
- Read a return policy and identify key information.
- Compare payment methods and discuss which feels safest and why.
- Role-play a buyer message that pressures a seller to move outside the platform.

  store website versus marketplace website.

- Sort websites into `retailer`, `specialist store`, or `marketplace`.
